Pick any positive whole number.

If it is even, divide it by 2.

If it is odd, multiply it by 3 and add 1.

Then repeat.

For example:

10 → 5 → 16 → 8 → 4 → 2 → 1

Try another number.

It also reaches 1.

Try millions of numbers.

They also reach 1.

Now comes the strange part.

Nobody has ever proved that this always happens.

This is the Collatz Conjecture, one of the most famous unsolved problems in mathematics. Proposed in 1937, it remains unproved despite decades of effort.

Curious about why such a simple problem remains unsolved, I started an independent computational mathematics research project and built a high-performance Collatz research platform in C++. The project has grown into 21 research modules, with experiments run on up to 50 million numbers.

Some findings so far:

• Stopping times are highly predictable. A drift-based model explains 97.62% of the variance in stopping times across 50 million integers (R² = 0.9762). One formula. One idea. Most of the mystery explained.

• Binary structure matters. Numbers ending in long runs of 1-bits consistently behave as the hardest residue classes, while alternating-bit patterns behave as the easiest. This held across every modulus tested from modulo 64 up to modulo 4096, verified at 7 successive levels.

• This isn't just observed — part of it is provable. For alternating-bit numbers like 21, 85, 341, applying the rule once produces a perfect power of 2, so the sequence collapses in a single odd step. The algebra reduces to two lines.

• The odd-to-odd dynamics are remarkably regular. Average logarithmic drift correlates above 0.97 with stopping-time difficulty across 1024 residue classes.

• The drift converges to a universal constant. As residue classes become finer (modulo 1024 → 32768), the average contraction rate stabilises at μ∞ ≈ −0.34921925, with total variation under 4 × 10⁻⁸ across five successive doublings of state space.

• The global transition statistics are almost perfectly memoryless. Large-scale Markov analysis across 50 million numbers shows P(v₂ = 1 | v₂ = 1) = 49.99%, within 0.01% of the theoretical prediction.

• Outliers reveal where the difficulty lives. Famous long trajectories such as 837,799 exhibit temporary persistence in low-division states, explaining unusually long paths — they are rare tail events, not exceptions to a different law.

• The reverse Collatz tree grows with remarkable consistency. Exploring backward from 1 to depth 70 produced over 40 million unique nodes, with an exponential growth constant of k ≈ 1.2637 and a least-squares fit of R² = 1.000000 over 50 depth levels.

None of this proves the Collatz Conjecture.

A proof would require fundamentally new mathematics.

But these experiments suggest that beneath the apparent chaos lies measurable and highly structured behaviour. The same statistical patterns emerge from multiple independent analyses.

The mystery remains.

And that's exactly what makes it worth studying.
